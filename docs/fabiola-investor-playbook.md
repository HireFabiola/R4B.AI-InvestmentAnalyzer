# Fabiola's Investment Playbook

## Purpose

This document captures my personal real estate investment decision-making process. It serves as the knowledge foundation for R4B.AI's agentic workflow.

Rather than teaching the AI generic real estate concepts, this playbook teaches it how **I** evaluate investment opportunities. As I complete more deals, this playbook will evolve, allowing R4B.AI to continually refine its reasoning.

---

# Opportunity Screening

## First Impressions

### What catches my attention first?

A listing with a thumbnail that reveals a home with charm, character, or visible potential.

### What makes me click on a listing?

If the exterior suggests the home has "good bones" or untapped potential, I want to learn more.

### What makes me immediately lose interest?

Properties that appear to require a complete gut renovation simply to become sellable.

### What makes me investigate further?

A home that appears structurally sound, reasonably clean, and has features I can imagine enhancing through thoughtful renovation.

### What information do I need before spending more than five minutes?

A quick comparison of the asking price against recent Zillow comparable sales to determine whether further analysis is worthwhile.

# Property Evaluation

## Exterior

The exterior is always the first photo I study.

### Features that immediately catch my attention

* Large windows
* Natural light
* Architectural character
* Spaciousness
* Attractive lot
* Interesting layout potential

### Signs of deferred maintenance that concern me

* Wood rot
* Old or damaged windows
* Visibly worn roofing
* Obvious plumbing issues
* Poor exterior maintenance

### Cosmetic issues that do not concern me

* Paint
* Flooring
* Appliances
* Cabinets
* Fixtures
* Landscaping

### Structural issues that immediately concern me

* Slanted floors
* Foundation movement
* Major structural cracking

Foundation issues are among my strongest deal breakers.

### Photos that encourage further investigation

Clean, well-lit photos that clearly show the property's condition and potential.

# Market & Comparable Analysis

## My first stop

Zillow is my primary starting point for comparable sales.

### What makes a property truly comparable?

* Similar bedroom count
* Similar bathroom count
* Within approximately 100–200 square feet
* Similar overall condition
* Same neighborhood whenever possible

### How important is the neighborhood?

Very important.

I avoid neighborhoods where safety concerns or widespread deterioration significantly reduce resale demand.

### Neighborhood characteristics I value

* Cleanliness
* Safety
* Consistent property maintenance
* Strong curb appeal
* Pride of ownership

### What makes me question an asking price?

When the asking price is not supported by either:

* Comparable sales
* The property's visible condition

### What signals an undervalued property?

A structurally sound property needing primarily cosmetic improvements that is priced approximately $20,000–$30,000 below comparable renovated homes.

# Financial Analysis

## First calculation

The first calculation I perform is the difference between:

* Asking Price
* Expected After Repair Value (ARV)

### Financial metrics that matter most

* Potential ROI
* Expected profit

### Metrics that are simply helpful

* Days on Market (DOM)

### Minimum financial requirements

A project should reasonably produce at least **$15,000–$20,000** in profit.

### When the numbers tell me to walk away

If projected profit falls below my minimum acceptable return after considering purchase price, renovation costs, holding costs, and selling expenses.

### Financial assumptions I am comfortable making

* My renovations will position the property toward the upper end of comparable sales.
* The property may remain on the market for up to three months after renovation.
* Preliminary repair estimates based on experience are sufficient until a contractor validates them.

---

# Repairs & Condition

## Repairs that concern me most

* Foundation
* Roof
* HVAC

These are high-cost systems that can significantly affect profitability.

### Repairs that do not intimidate me

Nearly all cosmetic renovations, including:

* Paint
* Flooring
* Kitchens
* Bathrooms
* Fixtures
* Landscaping

### Repairs I can estimate myself

Everything except:

* Foundation
* Roof
* HVAC

### When I involve a contractor

Once preliminary analysis suggests sufficient profit remains after estimated repairs.

### Repair-related deal breakers

Major foundation damage, severe roof issues, or HVAC replacement costs that eliminate my target profit.

### Managing unexpected repairs

Unexpected repairs should be anticipated through contingency planning and verified with contractor estimates before closing whenever possible.

# Risk Assessment

## Risks I accept

* Longer-than-expected selling timelines
* Minor repair surprises
* Moderate market fluctuations

### Risks I avoid

* Significant structural uncertainty
* Unknown foundation issues
* Repair costs that eliminate profitability
* Neighborhoods with weak resale demand

### My risk tolerance

Moderate.

I prefer predictable investments over speculative ones.

### Assumptions that must prove true

* Repair estimates remain reasonably accurate.
* Comparable sales support projected ARV.
* Market demand remains healthy.
* The renovation stays within budget.

### What could cause a deal to fail?

* Poor acquisition price
* Unexpected structural issues
* Significant budget overruns
* Incorrect ARV assumptions
* Poor pricing strategy after renovation

# Investment Strategy

## My current investment focus

My primary strategy is **fix and flip**.

Although I recognize when a property may better fit another strategy, I currently focus on identifying profitable renovation opportunities.

### Potential strategies considered

* Fix and Flip ✅
* Buy and Hold
* BRRRR
* Wholesale
* Pass

The AI should eventually recommend the strategy that best fits the opportunity—even if it differs from my current focus.

# Negotiation

## Determining my maximum offer

I consider:

* Days on Market
* Estimated repairs
* Desired profit
* Comparable sales

### When I negotiate

If the seller counters within approximately **$5,000** of my offer.

### When I walk away

* Seller expectations eliminate profitability.
* Updated information significantly changes repair costs.
* ROI no longer meets my minimum threshold.

### Negotiating factors that matter most

* Purchase price
* Inspection contingency
* Closing costs
* Due diligence period

### Information that strengthens my position

* Comparable sales
* Contractor estimates
* Time on Market
* Visible deferred maintenance
* Local market conditions

---

# Final Decision

## What gives me confidence?

* Strong comparable sales
* Manageable repair scope
* Good neighborhood
* Comfortable profit margin
* A renovation plan I clearly understand

### What still concerns me?

* Hidden repairs
* Market shifts
* Longer selling timelines

### Why am I saying "Yes"?

Because the opportunity creates value while maintaining an acceptable level of risk.

### Why am I saying "No"?

Because the risks outweigh the expected reward.

### Can I explain my decision?

If I cannot confidently explain why a deal works, I probably should not buy it.

### Primary exit strategy

Fix and Flip.

### Backup strategy

Evaluate whether the property can be held until market conditions improve.

* What did I learn?
* Which assumptions proved correct?
* Which assumptions were incorrect?
* What surprised me?
* Would I evaluate a similar property differently?
* What should R4B.AI learn from this experience?
* Should this experience improve my investment playbook?

Every property—whether purchased or rejected—becomes another lesson that strengthens both my investment philosophy and R4B.AI's future reasoning.
